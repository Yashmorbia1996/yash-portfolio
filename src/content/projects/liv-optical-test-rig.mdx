{/* ── THE PROBLEM ── */}

<div id="the-problem" class="not-prose mb-16 scroll-mt-28">
  <h2 class="text-4xl font-semibold tracking-[-0.04em] text-text-primary md:text-5xl lg:text-6xl">
    The problem
  </h2>
  <h3 class="mt-4 text-2xl font-semibold tracking-[-0.03em] text-text-primary md:text-3xl">
    No reliable way to screen laser engines before committing an entire batch
  </h3>
  <p class="mt-4 max-w-3xl text-base leading-relaxed text-text-secondary">
    The laser engine is the heart of the device. Before releasing a batch to the main production floor, we needed to verify that each engine met optical power and beam quality requirements. Without a fast, repeatable test method, the only option was slow manual spot-checking or releasing blind and catching failures downstream, which is expensive and disruptive in a regulated environment.
  </p>
  <p class="mt-4 max-w-3xl text-base leading-relaxed text-text-secondary">
    The existing flow was manual, operator-sensitive, and not built for batch volume. What we needed was an <strong class="text-text-primary">AQL-capable screening station</strong>: fast enough to run through a batch, consistent enough that the measurement itself wasn't introducing noise, and easy enough to load and unload that any trained technician could run it without risk of damaging the engine or skewing the reading.
  </p>
  <p class="mt-4 max-w-3xl text-base leading-relaxed text-text-secondary">
    There was also a second gap: we could measure total optical power (LIV), but we had no way to see <strong class="text-text-primary">where the beam was going</strong>. A good power number with a badly shaped beam can still fail in the device. Catching that required adding spatial imaging to the test station.
  </p>
</div>

  <h3 class="mt-4 text-2xl font-semibold tracking-[-0.03em] text-text-primary md:text-3xl">
    Power and spatial beam quality in one station
  </h3>
  <p class="mt-4 max-w-3xl text-base leading-relaxed text-text-secondary">
    Optical power alone does not tell the full story. An engine can hit its LIV spec while still having a beam that is poorly shaped, clipped, or asymmetric in a way that will cause problems downstream. To catch those issues before they reach the device, the third fixture added a <strong class="text-text-primary">fixed InGaAs camera</strong> mounted to the same reference frame as the LIV collection path.
  </p>
  <p class="mt-4 max-w-3xl text-base leading-relaxed text-text-secondary">
    The camera was aligned and bore-sighted to the engine so that every test produced both a power curve and a spatial beam image. That combination could catch aperture clipping, facet issues, and coupling anomalies that scalar power measurement alone would miss. This became the most comprehensive pre-release screening station in the clean room.
  </p>
  <p class="mt-3 max-w-3xl text-sm font-medium text-text-primary md:text-base">
    Released to production as the LIV and beam-imaging station. Full 39-part BOM with GD&T package, motion study, interference check, and exploded views completed for manufacturing and quality handoff.
  </p>
