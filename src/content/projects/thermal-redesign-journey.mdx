## Overview

A deployed Class II analyzer was generating thermal fault alarms at 3.2 per 100 units per month in the field. Root cause was traced to an underspecified thermal path between a power MOSFET and the chassis ground plane.

## Problem

The original design used a TO-220 MOSFET with a single-layer thermal pad and no heatsink. At 40°C ambient — common in clinical settings — junction temperature exceeded the 125°C limit under sustained load. Field service bulletins and firmware throttling were band-aids; a hardware fix was required.

## Approach

Thermal analysis in SolidWorks Simulation (cross-validated against an ANSYS Icepak model):

1. Modeled the baseline thermal path: junction → case → pad → PCB → chassis
2. Identified bottleneck: 8°C/W thermal resistance at the PCB-to-chassis interface caused by an undersized thermal via array
3. Evaluated three design alternatives: heatsink extrusion, copper slug insert, and via-in-pad redesign

Recommended solution: copper slug press-fitted into the PCB with direct chassis contact, plus re-routing of thermal vias to a 4×4 array pattern.

DFM review confirmed the slug was machinable at cost parity with the original BOM. ECO process completed in one revision cycle without requiring a new product verification build.

## Outcome

- Peak junction temperature: 89°C → 55°C (34°C reduction)
- Field thermal fault rate: 3.2 → 0.28 per 100 units/month (−91%)
- No firmware throttling required — full performance spec restored
- Solution adopted as the thermal design standard for the next-generation platform
